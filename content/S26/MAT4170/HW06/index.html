Exercise 3 [5]

Humans have determined all groups of order \(n < 2^{11}\) where we consider two groups to be equivalent if there is an isomorphism between them. There are $49\,487\,367\,289$ groups of order $2^{10} = 1024$ up to isomorphism, and it is an open question to determine the number of groups of order $2^{11} = 2048$.

Here, you will give examples of each of the groups of order \(n \leq 11\) up to isomorphism. You will need prove that each of the groups you find is not isomorphic to any of the others. It will probably be useful to keep in mind that the direct product $G_1 \times G_2$ is abelian if and only if $G_1$ and $G_2$ is abelian.

Give examples of each of the following:

  1. one group of order $1$;
  2. one group of order $p$ for each prime $p$;
  3. two groups of order $4$ (both abelian);
  4. two groups of order $6$ (one is abelian);
  5. five groups of order $8$ (three are abelian, and one group we saw on Exam 1);
  6. two groups of order $9$ (both are abelian); and
  7. two groups of order $10$ (one is abelian).
